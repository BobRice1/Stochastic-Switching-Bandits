\begin{abstract}
Adaptive decision-making in uncertain environments requires integrating reward history to guide flexible action selection. Beron et al. (2022) \cite{Beron2022} showed that mice performing a probabilistic two-choice bandit task exhibit stochastic, history-dependent switching behaviour that is accurately captured by reinforcement learning and recursive logistic regression models incorporating choice history bias (“stickiness”), whereas inference-based hidden Markov models (HMMs) exhibit excessive switching.
Here, we reproduce the key behavioural and generative modelling results under the original 80--20 reward condition and extend the analysis to a noisier 70--30 environment. We compare the behaviour of the recursive logistic regression (RFLR) model and HMM using reward-based performance metrics, including reward rate, switching frequency, and reward-per-switch efficiency, and examine how behavioural parameters adapt across environments.
We introduce nonlinear stickiness mechanisms by implementing temporally gated and prediction-error–dependent modulation of choice history bias and assess whether adaptive stickiness improves switching efficiency and behavioural stability. Model performance is evaluated using generative simulations and behavioural efficiency metrics.
Our results demonstrate that stickiness increases under environmental uncertainty and that adaptive stickiness mechanisms improve behavioural efficiency by reducing excessive switching while preserving reward. These findings suggest that stickiness functions as a dynamically regulated control variable that stabilises behaviour and supports efficient decision-making under uncertainty.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Adaptive decision-making in uncertain environments requires balancing behavioural flexibility with persistence. In probabilistic reversal learning tasks, agents must integrate reward history to infer changes in environmental contingencies while avoiding overreaction to stochastic outcomes. A central feature of such behaviour is stochastic switching, in which agents occasionally switch actions despite recent reward, reflecting uncertainty about the underlying state.
Beron et al. (2022) \cite{Beron2022} investigated this problem in mice performing a two-choice probabilistic bandit task and found that behaviour was best explained by models incorporating choice-history bias, or ``stickiness,'' rather than optimal latent-state inference. In particular, recursive logistic regression (RFLR) models captured the graded, stochastic switching observed in mice, whereas hidden Markov models (HMMs) exhibited excessive switching due to their sensitivity to stochastic reward omissions. These findings suggest that behavioural persistence plays a key role in stabilising decision-making under uncertainty.
However, the computational role of stickiness and its regulation across environments remains unclear. In particular, it is unknown whether stickiness is a fixed behavioural bias or an adaptive mechanism that adjusts to environmental uncertainty. Furthermore, it remains unclear whether dynamic modulation of stickiness could improve behavioural efficiency by balancing flexibility and stability.
In this study, we first reproduce the key behavioural and modelling results of Beron et al. (2022) under the original 80--20 reward condition. We then extend the analysis to a noisier 70--30 environment to examine how behavioural parameters adapt under increased uncertainty. Finally, we introduce nonlinear, adaptive stickiness mechanisms and evaluate their effects on switching behaviour and reward efficiency using generative simulations. These analyses provide new insight into the computational role of stickiness in adaptive decision-making under uncertainty.




\section{Methods: Reproduction of Beron et al. (2022)}
\subsection{Behavioural signature targeted for reproduction}
Beron et al. (2022) reported that mice performing a dynamic two-choice bandit task exhibit graded, stochastic switching rather than deterministic win--stay/lose--switch behaviour. The key behavioural signature is the conditional switching probability $P(\mathrm{switch}\mid\mathrm{history})$, which increases with accumulating negative evidence but remains bounded below one. Despite stochastic feedback, mice achieve near-maximal reward rates, indicating efficient adaptation in uncertain environments.
\subsection{Reproduction workflow and data provenance}
To reproduce the principal findings, we used the publicly available dataset and analysis/model code released by Beron et al. (2022). Reproduction consisted of running the authors' pipeline to regenerate the key behavioural summaries and model-comparison figures reported in the original study. All reproduced figures were generated by executing the provided scripts/notebooks with fixed random seeds where applicable, and outputs were cross-checked against the paper for qualitative agreement (e.g., graded policy structure, model ranking, and HMM overswitching).
\subsection{Computational models considered in Beron et al. (2022)}
Beron et al. (2022) compared multiple model classes that differ in how reward history and environmental uncertainty are represented. We summarise these model families here to define the computational hypotheses evaluated in both the reproduction and extension analyses.
\paragraph{\textbf{Win--Stay/Lose--Switch (WSLS).}}
A heuristic strategy that repeats rewarded actions and switches after unrewarded outcomes. WSLS predicts near-deterministic switching after losses and therefore fails to capture graded behavioural responses.
\paragraph{\textbf{Reinforcement learning (RL).}}
A model-free approach that incrementally updates action values via reward prediction errors, typically paired with stochastic action selection. RL captures gradual adaptation but does not explicitly represent latent states of the environment.
\paragraph{\textbf{Inference-based models (HMM).}}
A latent-state model that infers the current reward contingency from past outcomes using Bayesian belief updating. Inference models can adapt rapidly following reversals but may over-switch under stochastic feedback if losses are over-interpreted as evidence for state change. Beliefs are updated according to:
\[
P(s_t \mid o_{1:t}) \propto P(o_t \mid s_t)\sum_{s_{t-1}} P(s_t \mid s_{t-1})P(s_{t-1} \mid o_{1:t-1}),
\]
where $P(s_t \mid o_{1:t})$ is the posterior belief over latent reward states given observations $o_{1:t}$.
For Sticky HMM variants, $\mathrm{PE}_t$ was defined as the difference between the observed reward and the belief-weighted expected reward under the model's current posterior belief and emission probabilities.
\paragraph{\textbf{Recursive logistic regression (RFLR).}}
A reduced model that predicts choices from accumulated choice--outcome history while incorporating a choice-history bias (stickiness). In the recursive formulation, the probability of choosing action $a_t$ is:
\[
P(a_t = 1)=\frac{1}{1+\exp\left[-(\beta x_t + \alpha a_{t-1})\right]},
\]
where $x_t$ represents accumulated reward evidence, $\beta$ is an inverse temperature controlling sensitivity to evidence, and $\alpha$ is the stickiness parameter biasing repetition of the previous choice.
Additional analysis scripts were used to regenerate behavioural summary metrics reported in the original study, including conditional switching probabilities, event-triggered switching dynamics, and quantitative comparisons between model and mouse policies. These analyses reproduced the key behavioural signatures described by Beron et al. (2022).





\section{Methods: Extensions}
\subsection{Behaviour under increased uncertainty (70--30 condition)}
\paragraph{\textbf{Generative model simulations.}}
Using cross-validated parameter estimates, we generated synthetic behaviour in both reward environments (80-20 and 70-30) under three model classes: RFLR, Ideal HMM (Thompson sampling), and Sticky HMM. To ensure identical reversal structure across models, simulations were run on the empirical latent state sequences extracted from the experimental sessions. Reward delivery probabilities were set to 0.8/0.2 (80--20) or 0.7/0.3 (70--30). Transition probabilities were set to the task transition parameter used in the generative simulations ($p_{\mathrm{switch}}$), consistent with the probabilistic bandit structure. For each environment, RFLR parameters (\(\alpha\), \(\beta\), \(\tau\)) estimated via cross-validation were used to simulate behaviour. To assess parameter generalisation across environments, we additionally simulated the 70-30 condition using parameters fitted in the 80-20 environment. 
\paragraph{\textbf{Cross-validated parameter estimation across reward environments.}}
To quantify how behavioural parameters depend on environmental uncertainty, the RFLR model was fit separately in the 80--20 and 70--30 reward environments. Parameter estimation was performed using 5-fold cross-validation at the session level to ensure robust generalisation and prevent information leakage between training and evaluation data. Sessions were randomly partitioned into five folds using a fixed random seed. For each fold, model parameters $(\alpha, \beta, \tau)$ were fit on four training folds by maximising the log-likelihood of the observed choice sequence under the RFLR model. Optimisation was implemented using stochastic gradient descent (10{,}000 iterations; learning rate = 0.01). The fitted parameters were then evaluated on the held-out fold to assess out-of-sample predictive performance. This procedure was repeated for all folds, and the final parameter estimate for each environment was taken as the mean of parameter estimates across folds.
\paragraph{\textbf{Behavioural metrics and model comparison.}}
Model behaviour was evaluated using summary statistics computed at the session level. For each simulated session, we computed the total reward obtained per session and the total number of switches per session. These metrics were used to characterise the reward-cognitive trade-off across models. To quantify switching dynamics around environmental reversals, we computed the probability of switching as a function of trial distance relative to block transitions. Switch probabilities were aligned to empirical state transitions and averaged across sessions to characterise cognitive lag and adaptation dynamics. Finally, parameter estimates (\(\alpha\), \(\beta\), \(\tau\)) were compared across the 80-20 and 70-30 environments to assess systematic shifts associated with increased reward uncertainty. 

\subsection{Nonlinear stickiness mechanisms}
To extend the fixed-stickiness framework, we implemented dynamic modulation of the stickiness parameter ($\alpha$) based on recent environmental feedback. These mechanisms were applied to the RFLR, HMM, and forgetting Q-learning (F-Q) architectures and evaluated using generative simulations.
\paragraph{Rolling window statistic}
Let $W_t$ denote the set of indices corresponding to the most recent $N$ trials up to time $t$ (or fewer if $t<N$).
We define the rolling statistic as
\begin{equation}
\bar{E}_t = \frac{1}{|W_t|}\sum_{k\in W_t} \mathrm{PE}_k.
\end{equation}
This statistic was updated sequentially on each trial using a fixed-length rolling buffer.
\paragraph{\textbf{Informational gating: signed prediction-error modulation (RFLR and HMM).}}
For the RFLR and HMM models, stickiness was dynamically scaled as a function of recent signed prediction error (PE).
On each trial $t>0$, effective stickiness was computed via exponential gating:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_t = \alpha_{\text{base}} \exp\!\left(\gamma\,\bar{E}_t\right),
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_{\text{base}}$ is baseline stickiness, $\gamma$ is surprise sensitivity, and $\bar{E}_t$ is the mean PE over an $N$-trial rolling window (defined below).

\paragraph{Prediction-error signals.}
Prediction-error signals were defined to match the implementation for each model class. 
For dynamic RFLR, we used an internal expected-reward proxy $\hat r_t=\sigma(\phi\,c_t)$ derived from the model's choice-weighted term $c_t$, and defined
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{PE}_t \;=\; r_t - \hat r_t,
\qquad \mathrm{PE}_0 \;=\; r_0 - 0.5.
\end{equation}
For Sticky HMM variants, $\mathrm{PE}_t$ was defined as the difference between the observed reward and the belief-weighted expected reward under the model's current posterior belief and emission probabilities. In the nonlinear Sticky HMM implementation, emission probabilities were parameterised asymmetrically with respect to chosen action, such that the reward likelihood under each latent state depended explicitly on the action taken at trial $t$. The belief-weighted expected reward was therefore computed using this action-conditioned emission structure, consistent with the computational implementation.
For the F-Q nonlinear stickiness variant, the gating signal used the absolute value error
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon_t \;=\; \bigl|\,r_t - Q_t(a_t)\,\bigr|,
\end{equation}
and the rolling window statistic was defined as
\begin{equation}
\bar{E}_t = \frac{1}{|W_t|}\sum_{k\in W_t} \varepsilon_k,
\end{equation}
where $W_t$ denotes the set of indices corresponding to the most recent $N$ trials (or fewer if $t<N$).
This rolling mean was updated sequentially at each trial and used to modulate stickiness via exponential gating.

\subsection{Multi-level gating framework and optimal evaluation}
\paragraph{Model hypotheses.}
Phase 4 evaluates distinct computational hypotheses for how agents use environmental ``surprise'' to regulate choice persistence in the 2ABT. We compare (i) a direct policy model where surprise gates motor persistence trial-by-trial (dynamic RFLR), (ii) a belief-based model where Bayesian state inference is combined with motor-level stickiness (Sticky HMM), and (iii) a temporal/value baseline where switching behaviour emerges from windowed value dynamics (FQ value-gated). In addition, belief-reset variants test whether omission-driven surprise triggers change-point detection and belief reinitialisation.
\paragraph{Cross-validated optimisation.}
To prevent overfitting, parameters were fit using 5-fold cross-validation over sessions. For each fold, model parameters were optimised on the training set and evaluated on held-out sessions using mean log-likelihood (Mean LL) as the primary performance metric.
\paragraph{Nested search over temporal parameters.}
Discrete temporal parameters (e.g., commitment window $N$) were selected via a nested search procedure. The outer loop enumerated candidate $N$ values, and the inner loop performed continuous optimisation of model parameters (e.g., $\alpha_{\mathrm{base}}$ and surprise sensitivity $\gamma$) using L-BFGS-B with bounds. Parameter stability was assessed via across-fold variability, and the optimal $N$ was selected via plurality vote across folds.

\paragraph{\textbf{Model design: temporal gating and surprise-dependent modulation.}}
To extend the computational framework beyond fixed stickiness, we implemented nonlinear stickiness mechanisms that dynamically regulate behavioural persistence. These extensions were applied to RFLR, HMM, and forgetting Q-learning (F-Q) models. Temporal gating was implemented at the value level within the F-Q architecture. Q-values were held fixed during each $N$-trial window and updated only at window boundaries using the mean reward obtained for each action during that window. The agent continued to make a free stochastic choice on every trial based on the current (potentially frozen) Q-values, so temporal gating discretised the learning signal rather than the motor output. Informational gating was implemented via prediction-error--dependent modulation of stickiness. For RFLR and HMM variants, the gating signal used \emph{signed} prediction error $\mathrm{PE}_t$. For the F-Q nonlinear stickiness variant, the gating signal used \emph{absolute} value error $\varepsilon_t=\lvert r_t - Q_t(a_t)\rvert$.
\paragraph{\textbf{Simulation protocol and recorded outputs.}}
Synthetic behavioural sessions were generated using the same probabilistic bandit task structure described above. Models were simulated across multiple sessions, and we recorded the dynamic stickiness trace, switching frequency, and (where applicable) value estimates (e.g., Q-values for F-Q). These outputs were used to assess how adaptive stickiness regulates the balance between persistence and flexibility under stochastic feedback.
\paragraph{\textbf{Implementation details.}}
All extension simulations were implemented in Python using custom code written for this project. For nonlinear stickiness experiments, synthetic behavioural data were generated for 5 independent sessions of 120 trials each, with reward contingencies evolving according to a two-state Markov process with transition probability $P(\mathrm{switch})=0.1$. Behavioural metrics were averaged across sessions. Dynamic stickiness was modulated according to the mean prediction error over an N-step rolling window, with parameter values matched to those used in the computational implementation. Temporal gating was implemented by discretising Q-value updates into $N$-trial windows, with Q-values held fixed within each window and updated using the mean reward per action at window boundaries, while informational gating was implemented by updating the stickiness parameter on each trial according to the accumulated prediction error window. We hypothesised that dynamic stickiness would improve switching efficiency by preventing excessive switching in response to stochastic reward fluctuations and, where evaluated against baseline models, improve alignment with mouse switching dynamics.

\subsection{Cross-validated predictive comparison of adaptive stickiness models}
To complement reward--switch efficiency analyses, we compared adaptive model variants using held-out predictive
log-likelihood from 5-fold cross-validation (Table~\ref{tab:phase4_model_comparison}).
Across both reward environments, dynamic RFLR achieved the highest predictive performance (least negative Mean LL; Table~I). In the 80–20 condition, dynamic RFLR improved held-out log-likelihood by approximately 0.0028 per trial relative to the next-best adaptive variant (HMM resetv2), and by 0.0056 per trial in the 70–30 condition. Although modest at the single-trial level, these differences accumulate across sessions and indicate consistently superior out-of-sample predictive performance. Parameter estimates also showed systematic
context-dependence: in dynamic RFLR, base stickiness increased from $\alpha_{\text{base}}=1.052\pm0.010$ in 80--20
to $\alpha_{\text{base}}=1.352\pm0.020$ in 70--30, consistent with greater behavioural persistence under increased
reward stochasticity. In contrast, surprise sensitivity decreased from $\gamma=0.680\pm0.020$ (80--20) to
$\gamma=0.506\pm0.010$ (70--30), suggesting reduced trial-by-trial reactivity under higher uncertainty.
Across models, the selected commitment window was typically short ($N=1$), with value-gated FQ favouring a slightly
longer window ($N=2$), consistent with discretised value updating.
Together, these results provide likelihood-based support for adaptive stickiness mechanisms and quantify how fitted
persistence parameters shift with environmental uncertainty.

\subsection*{Normative interpretation: efficiency versus optimal inference}
To contextualise adaptive stickiness mechanisms, we compared their behaviour to an inference-based benchmark (Ideal HMM with Thompson sampling), which approximates normative Bayesian state inference in the probabilistic bandit task. Although the Ideal HMM achieved marginally higher total reward (Fig.~6), it did so at the cost of substantially increased switching frequency, reflecting heightened sensitivity to stochastic reward omissions. 
In contrast, dynamic RFLR achieved comparable reward while maintaining lower switching rates and improved reward-per-switch efficiency. This pattern suggests that adaptive stickiness does not maximise reward in a strictly normative sense, but instead optimises a trade-off between reward acquisition and switching cost. In environments with stochastic feedback, excessive reactivity can degrade behavioural efficiency even if it remains inference-consistent.
From this perspective, stickiness can be interpreted as a bounded-rational control mechanism: rather than implementing full latent-state inference, agents may regulate behavioural persistence to stabilise decisions under uncertainty. In this sense, stickiness may serve as a low-dimensional control signal that approximates belief updating without explicitly representing latent states. Adaptive modulation of stickiness allows persistence to scale with environmental volatility, increasing when feedback becomes unreliable and thereby preventing over-switching. 
Thus, dynamic stickiness may approximate inference-optimal behaviour in a resource-efficient manner, sacrificing minimal reward while substantially reducing switching cost. This interpretation reframes stickiness not as a suboptimal bias, but as an efficiency-oriented computational strategy for balancing flexibility and stability in uncertain environments.



\section*{Discussion}
The central contribution of this work is to demonstrate that stickiness is not merely a descriptive bias parameter, but functions as a dynamically regulated control variable that shapes behavioural efficiency and predictive performance under uncertainty.

\subsection*{Reproduction and validation of stochastic switching}
We first successfully reproduced the principal behavioural and modelling findings of Beron et al. (2022). Consistent with the original study, mouse behaviour exhibited graded, stochastic switching that depended systematically on recent reward history. Among the tested models, the RFLR framework most accurately captured both the conditional switching structure and reversal-aligned adaptation dynamics, whereas inference-based HMM variants exhibited excessive switching due to sensitivity to stochastic reward omissions. This reproduction establishes a validated computational baseline for examining how behavioural persistence adapts to environmental volatility.

\subsection*{Stickiness increases with environmental uncertainty}
Extending the task to the noisier 70–30 environment revealed systematic modulation of fitted parameters. Cross-validated estimates (Table~I) showed that base stickiness increased from $\alpha_{\text{base}} = 1.052 \pm 0.010$ in 80–20 to $\alpha_{\text{base}} = 1.352 \pm 0.020$ in 70–30, representing an approximate 28\% increase in behavioural persistence under increased reward stochasticity. In contrast, surprise sensitivity decreased from $\gamma = 0.680 \pm 0.020$ to $\gamma = 0.506 \pm 0.010$, suggesting reduced trial-by-trial reactivity in noisier environments. 
Importantly, these shifts were accompanied by consistent cross-validated improvements in predictive log-likelihood for dynamic RFLR relative to alternative adaptive variants across both environments. Thus, increased stickiness under uncertainty is not merely descriptively efficient but also statistically supported by improved out-of-sample prediction.

\subsection*{Adaptive stickiness improves efficiency and predictive performance}
Generative analyses demonstrated that adaptive modulation of stickiness improves reward-per-switch efficiency by reducing excessive switching while preserving reward acquisition. Crucially, likelihood-based comparison confirms that these adaptive mechanisms also enhance predictive fidelity to observed choice sequences. Across both environments, dynamic RFLR achieved the highest held-out log-likelihood among adaptive variants (Table~I), indicating that trial-by-trial modulation of persistence provides a better account of behavioural structure than static or inference-only models.
Together, the generative and predictive results converge on the interpretation that adaptive stickiness stabilises behaviour in noisy environments without sacrificing flexibility. Rather than implementing full latent-state inference, agents may approximate efficient adaptation through local modulation of behavioural persistence.

\subsection*{Stickiness as a computational control variable}
These findings support a reinterpretation of stickiness as a functional control parameter regulating the flexibility–stability trade-off. Fixed stickiness improves behavioural stability but cannot adjust across environments. Dynamic stickiness, by contrast, allows persistence to scale with environmental uncertainty, increasing when stochasticity is high and decreasing when feedback is reliable. This mechanism provides a computationally efficient alternative to full Bayesian inference while preserving key behavioural signatures observed in mice.

\subsection*{Limitations and future directions}
Several limitations remain. First, although adaptive variants were evaluated using cross-validated likelihood, more comprehensive hierarchical fitting across animals would strengthen statistical inference. Second, simulations were conducted over limited session numbers, which may constrain statistical power. Finally, the present framework does not incorporate neural implementation constraints. Future work should examine whether adaptive stickiness mechanisms can be embedded within biologically plausible circuit models and whether similar persistence modulation is observable at the neural level.

\section*{Conclusion}
This study reproduced and extended prior work on stochastic switching in probabilistic bandit tasks and demonstrated that stickiness plays a fundamental computational role in adaptive decision-making. Under increased environmental uncertainty, stickiness increased systematically and was associated with improved cross-validated predictive performance. Adaptive, surprise-dependent modulation of stickiness improved behavioural efficiency, stabilised reversal dynamics, and yielded the strongest out-of-sample likelihood across model variants.
These findings suggest that dynamic regulation of behavioural persistence represents a general computational principle for balancing flexibility and stability in uncertain environments. Rather than relying on full latent-state inference, agents may achieve efficient adaptation through local modulation of stickiness, providing a parsimonious and predictive account of stochastic switching behaviour.
